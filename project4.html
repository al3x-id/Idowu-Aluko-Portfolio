Amazon Sales & Payment Behavior Analysis - SQL Project

Project Overview

This project analyzes Amazon sales data using structured SQL queries to explore revenue trends, product category performance, geographic distribution of sales, and profitability. The aim is to understand how sales fluctuate across different regions, time periods, and product types.

Objectives

1. Write queries to generate metrics for a monthly sales dashboard:

  • Total Sales per Month, Total Orders per Month & Average Order Value.
  • Best-Selling Categories & Products.

2. Analyse customer behavior

  • Top 5 customers by total spend.
  • Number of orders per customer.
  • Average purchase value per customer
  • Repeat vs. One-time customers

3. Analyse product/category-level performance

  • Most profitable categories.
  • Products with the Highest Quantity Sold.
  • Products with High Cancellation Rates.

4. Breakdown sales by customer location

  • Top 5 cities by sales volume.
  • Regional preference for product categories.
  • City-wise revenue trend over time.

5. Performance of different payment methods

  • Payment method preference per region.
  • Success vs cancellation rates by payment method.
  • Indicate if success vs cancellation rate is high or low.
  • Correlation between payment method and order status.

6. Identify trends and seasonality

  • Sales trend over weeks/months.
  • Sales peaks and dips.
  • Impact of weekday/weekend on order volume.

Cleaning Process

To ensure accurate analysis and proper formatting, a structured data cleaning process was carried out on the "amazon_sales2025" dataset. Below is a breakdown of each transformation step, including the purpose and outcome.

1. View Raw Data


SELECT * FROM amazon_sales2025;
  • This query displays the original dataset to examine its structure, identify inconsistencies, and understand the data types before making any changes.

2. Create a Backup of the Original Data


CREATE TABLE amazon_sales_2025 LIKE amazon_sales2025;
INSERT amazon_sales_2025 SELECT * FROM amazon_sales2025;
  • To preserve the raw data, I created a copy of the table named amazon_sales_2025. This ensures that the original dataset remains untouched in case I need to revert or cross-check values.

3. Clean, Update and Standardize "Order id" Column 


UPDATE amazon_sales_2025
SET `order id` = CAST(SUBSTRING(`Order id`, 4, 4) AS UNSIGNED);
  • Some order IDs likely contain prefixes or unwanted characters. This step extracts the numeric part (4 characters starting from the 4th character) and converts it into a number for consistent formatting and easier analysis.

4. Convert order id Column Type


ALTER TABLE amazon_sales_2025
MODIFY COLUMN `order id` INT;
  • Once cleaned, the order id column is changed to an integer data type, allowing for faster operations and numeric filtering during analysis.

5. Standardize date Column Format


UPDATE amazon_sales_2025
SET `date` = STR_TO_DATE(`date`, '%d-%m-%Y');
  • Checks the format of the date values and attempts to convert them from string format (dd-mm-yyyy) to SQL's DATE format using STR_TO_DATE().
  • This transformation updates all rows to reflect the cleaned date format, making them suitable for date-based queries like filtering, sorting, and aggregating.

6. Convert date Column Type to DATE


ALTER TABLE amazon_sales_2025
MODIFY COLUMN `date` DATE;
  • Changes the column data type from string to DATE, improving efficiency for time-series operations and ensuring date integrity across SQL operations.

SQL Analysis & Dashboard Metrics

Monthly Sales Dashboard Metrics

Metric Calculated:

  • Monthly revenue
  • Number of orders per month
  • Average Order Value (AOV)

SELECT
  DATE_FORMAT(`date`, '%Y-%m') AS `Month`, 
  SUM(`total sales`) AS Monthly_sales,
  COUNT(`order id`) AS Total_orders,
  ROUND(SUM(`total sales`)/COUNT(`order id`), 2) AS AVG_order
FROM amazon_sales_2025
GROUP BY `Month`;
  • DATE_FORMAT(date, '%Y-%m'): Converts the date to month-year format.
  • SUM(total sales): Total revenue in the month.
  • COUNT(order id): Number of orders placed.
  • AVG_order: Revenue divided by number of orders = average order value (AOV).

Insights:

  • February (2025-02) had the highest revenue despite fewer orders than March, suggesting that customers made higher-value purchases that month. The AOV is the highest at ₦1,085.80.
  • March (2025-03) saw the most orders (131) but slightly lower total revenue, leading to a lower AOV than February. This may indicate smaller or more frequent purchases.
  • April (2025-04) experienced a sharp drop in sales and orders, with only 6 orders totaling ₦3,420. This could indicate:
    • Inventory or logistics issues
    • Seasonality or lower demand
    • A data reporting gap (e.g., incomplete data)

Average Purchase Value per Customer


SELECT `Customer name`,
SUM(`Total sales`) AS Total_spend,
COUNT(`Order id`) AS No_of_order,
ROUND(SUM(`Total sales`)/COUNT(`Order id`), 2) AS APV
FROM amazon_sales_2025
GROUP BY `Customer name`;
  • Combines spend and frequency to calculate Average Purchase Value (APV).

Insights:

  • The data shows that Olivia Wilson stands out as the most valuable customer, with the highest total spend (₦36,170) and the highest average per-order value (₦1,247.24), reflecting premium purchasing behavior. In contrast, Emma Clark places the most orders (32), indicating high engagement, though her average per-order value (₦928.13) is moderate. Other customers like Jane Smith, John Doe, and Emily Johnson also show strong spending power, each maintaining an APV above ₦1,000. Meanwhile, Sophia Miller has both the lowest total spend and one of the lowest APVs, suggesting an opportunity for targeted campaigns to boost engagement and order value. Overall, while high-frequency and high-spend customers contribute significantly to revenue, strategies to encourage less active or lower-spending customers could further drive growth.

Repeat vs. One-time Customers

CASE
	WHEN COUNT(`Order id`) = 1 THEN 'One-time customer'

GROUP BY `Customer name`;

Insights:

The top three most active repeat customers are Emma Clark (32 orders), Jane Smith (30 orders), and Olivia Wilson (29 orders), showing strong purchasing engagement. Even the least active repeat customer, Sophia Miller, has still made 16 orders, indicating a healthy base of loyal customers who consistently return to buy more.
  • Overall, this suggests the business has successfully cultivated a core group of loyal repeat buyers, which is a positive indicator for customer retention and potential lifetime value.

WITH Pref_CTE AS(
SELECT
`Customer Location`,
Category,
SUM(`Total Sales`) AS Total_sales,
RANK() OVER(PARTITION BY `Customer Location` ORDER BY SUM(`Total Sales`) DESC) AS Preference
FROM amazon_sales_2025
GROUP BY `Customer Location`, Category)
SELECT `Customer Location`,
Category
FROM Pref_CTE
WHERE Preference = 1;
  • Uses a CTE and Window Function (RANK()) to find top-selling category in each location.

Insights:

  • The Electronics category is sold in 7 out of 10 cities: Boston, Chicago, Houston, Los Angeles, Miami, New York, and San Francisco, showing widespread demand across major urban markets.
  • Home Appliances are sold only in 3 cities: Dallas, Denver, and Seattle, suggesting a more localized or niche market presence.

Key Insights:

  • Electronics clearly have broader geographic appeal and stronger distribution, likely driving higher overall sales volume and revenue.
  • Home Appliances are concentrated in fewer cities, which could mean there’s room to expand into more locations, or that demand is more regional and focused.
  • Strategic decisions could include reinforcing electronics promotions nationwide, while testing targeted marketing to grow Home Appliances in new cities.

    Preferred Payment by Region

`Payment Method`,
COUNT(`Order id`) AS No_of_order,

    Key Insights:

    • PayPal and Credit Card together dominate most cities, highlighting them as crucial for checkout optimization and promotions.
    • Debit Card usage in select cities shows an opportunity for targeted discounts or messaging focused on budget-conscious customers.
    • Amazon Pay shows very limited use; marketing efforts may be needed if the business wants to grow that channel.

    Success vs Cancellation Rate

    
SELECT `Payment Method`,
COUNT(*) AS status_rate,
ROUND(SUM(CASE
  WHEN `status` = 'Completed' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) * 100 / COUNT(*), 2) AS Success_rate,
ROUND(SUM(CASE
  WHEN `status` = 'Cancelled' THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) * 100 / COUNT(*), 2) AS Cancellation_rate
FROM amazon_sales_2025
WHERE `status` IN('Completed', 'Cancelled')
GROUP BY `Payment Method`;
    • Shows percentage of successful vs cancelled transactions by payment method.

    Insights:

    • Amazon Pay has the highest success rate at 75% and the lowest cancellation rate (25%), though it’s used less frequently (28 transactions).
    • PayPal also performs reasonably well, with a success rate of 65.22% across 46 transactions, making it both popular and fairly reliable.
    • Gift Card shows a very low success rate of 25% and the highest cancellation rate (75%), suggesting significant issues possibly with redemption, card balance errors, or fraud checks.
    • Credit Card and Debit Card both have more failures than successes:
      • Credit Card: 51.52% success vs. 48.48% cancellation.
      • Debit Card: only 41.18% success and 58.82% cancellation, making it particularly problematic.

    Key Insights:

    • Focus on promoting and optimizing Amazon Pay and PayPal, as they deliver the best reliability.
    • nvestigate why Gift Card, Debit Card, and Credit Card have high cancellation rates, this could be due to payment gateway issues, fraud detection settings, or customer errors.
    • Consider customer support messaging or checkout flow improvements to reduce friction for lower-performing methods.

    Success vs Cancellation Rate Indicator

    
WITH PM_CTE AS
(SELECT `Payment Method`,
`status`,
COUNT(`status`),
RANK() OVER(PARTITION BY `Payment Method` ORDER BY COUNT(`status`) DESC) AS status_rank
FROM amazon_sales_2025
WHERE `status` IN('Completed', 'Cancelled')
GROUP BY `Payment Method`, `status`)
SELECT `Payment Method`,
CASE
  WHEN `status` = 'Completed' AND status_rank = 1 THEN 'High'
  ELSE 'Low'
END AS Success_rate,
CASE
  WHEN `status` = 'Cancelled' AND status_rank = 1 THEN 'High'
  ELSE 'Low'
END AS Cancellation_rate
FROM PM_CTE
WHERE status_rank = 1;
    • Flags if a payment method has a high or low success/cancellation rate.

    Insights:

    • Amazon Pay, PayPal, and Credit Card all have high success rates and low cancellation rates, making them the most reliable options for ensuring successful transactions and smooth customer experience.
    • Debit Card and Gift Card show low success rates and high cancellation rates, highlighting them as riskier choices that could lead to more failed checkouts and dissatisfied customers.

    Key Insights:

    • The business should prioritize and promote Amazon Pay, PayPal, and Credit Card at checkout to maximize transaction success.
    • Investigate and address issues affecting Debit Card and Gift Card payments whether technical (gateway errors) or operational (balance or fraud checks) to reduce cancellations and improve overall checkout performance.

    Sales Peaks and Dips

    
CREATE TEMPORARY TABLE Sales_Trend AS
SELECT `date`,
COUNT(`Total Sales`) AS Sales
FROM amazon_sales_2025
GROUP BY `date`;

-- Peaks
SELECT * FROM Sales_Trend
ORDER BY Sales DESC
LIMIT 5;

-- Dips
SELECT * FROM Sales_Trend
ORDER BY Sales ASC
LIMIT 5;
    • Identifies days with highest and lowest sales after storing daily totals in a temp table.

    Insights:

    • The peaks are clustered mostly in February and March 2025, matching the previously observed seasonal strength.
    • February 2025 had three of the top five peak days (Feb 10, Feb 20, Feb 23), showing it was an especially strong month with frequent sales spikes.
    • March 2025 had two peak days (Mar 14, Mar 24), keeping momentum into the next month.
    • Four of the five lowest sales days occurred in March and February, the same months that also saw peaks — indicating volatility rather than steady demand.
    • Dips on consecutive days (Mar 27 & Mar 28) suggest a brief period of very low customer activity, possibly due to external factors (holidays, technical issues, or supply delays).
    • Despite overall strong months, these isolated dips reveal gaps that might be related to operational downtime, campaign pauses, or customer fatigue after earlier peaks.

    Key Insights:

    • These peaks likely align with marketing campaigns, product launches, or seasonal demand and identifying what drove them can help replicate similar results in future months (especially outside the seasonal window).
    • Understanding what happened on these specific low-sales days can help avoid future dips by improving marketing consistency, inventory management, or addressing timing issues around weekends or holidays.

    Weekday vs Weekend Sales

    
SELECT 
CASE 
  WHEN DAYOFWEEK(`date`) IN (1, 7) THEN 'Weekends'
  ELSE 'Weekdays'
END AS day_type,
COUNT(`Order id`) AS Order_count,
SUM(`Total Sales`) AS Total_sales
FROM amazon_sales_2025
GROUP BY CASE 
  WHEN DAYOFWEEK(`date`) IN (1, 7) THEN 'Weekends'
  ELSE 'Weekdays'
END;
    • Splits total sales and order counts by weekdays vs weekends.

    Insights:

    • Weekdays account for the bulk of activity, with 182 orders generating ₦186,085 in total sales.
    • Weekends have far fewer orders (68 orders) and significantly lower total sales (₦57,760).

    Observations:

    • Average sales per order are similar, but total weekday volume is much higher likely due to regular shopping patterns, promotions, or business purchases.
    • Weekends see a sharp drop in both order volume and revenue, suggesting lower customer engagement or limited promotions.

    Key Insights:

    • The business performs strongly on weekdays; weekends underperform in both order count and revenue.
    • To balance demand, the business could introduce weekend-specific offers, flash sales, or targeted ads to boost weekend sales.

    • Average order value peaked in March 2025, indicating high-value transactions. Order count, however, was highest in February.
